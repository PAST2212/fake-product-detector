# Catching Fakes at Scale: How I Built an AI-Powered Counterfeit Detector for Amazon and OTTO.de

*How a decade of marketplace fraud work and four peer-reviewed papers collapsed into a multi-agent system that costs pennies to run.*

---

> **TL;DR**
> - **What it does.** One command (`/investigate`) orchestrates five specialist AI agents across Amazon and OTTO.de — listing, reviews, images, seller, ethics — and emits a cited, schema-validated counterfeit verdict plus an HTML report.
> - **What it costs.** Roughly 200,000 tokens per full investigation. Cents per listing at scale.
> - **What it caught.** A €19 Amazon.de tankini with identical photography live on AliExpress ($6.59), DHgate, SHEIN, and Alibaba. Composite score 0.85, **LIKELY COUNTERFEIT**, risk **HIGH** — in 12 minutes.
> - **Why it matters now.** Under EU Digital Services Act Article 30, marketplaces must prove reasonable efforts against counterfeit listings. This is that proof, runnable per listing, grounded in four peer-reviewed papers, and MIT-licensed.

## A €19 tankini on Amazon.de

Recently I ran my detector against this listing on amazon.de:

> **ZWDC Bikini Set Damen Zweiteilig V-Ausschnitt Raffung Badeanzug mit Rock Bauchweg Bademode Sexy Strandmode Schwimmset Gepolstert Ohne Bügel Triangel Top für Sommer Strand Urlaub Strandparty** — €18,98

Twelve minutes of orchestrated AI work later, the verdict came back: **LIKELY COUNTERFEIT, composite score 0.85, risk HIGH.** The evidence was damning — identical product photography was live on AliExpress at $6.59, on DHgate, on SHEIN, and on an Alibaba CDN. The "brand" ZWDC has no website. The manufacturer field on the listing literally read *"Damen Bademode"* (German for "women's swimwear"). The seller — a Shenzhen entity calling itself `ZWDC ❥50%~80% Rabatt, Überraschung Ladens❥` — was simultaneously selling bikinis, fleece jackets, push-up bras, and rain ponchos from the same 558-SKU catalog.

That whole investigation cost roughly 200,000 tokens across five Claude subagents. At scale, that is cents per product.

This is a system that did not exist when I needed it most.

## Where this comes from

For years I worked in **Fraud Management at OTTO Group** in Hamburg — one of Europe's largest e-commerce retailers — on the team responsible for detecting non-compliant and counterfeit sellers on the marketplace. The process we used back then was, frankly, brutal:

- **Manual review queues.** Analysts worked through flagged listings one by one.
- **External supplier workflows.** We paid specialist fraud vendors per-seller for background checks that took days.
- **Reactive, not proactive.** Most counterfeit detection happened *after* a brand holder complained — not before a customer was deceived.
- **Costly.** Each deep investigation represented hours of human labor plus vendor fees. Scaling across tens of thousands of sellers was economically impossible without strong upstream filtering.

What we built worked, but it did not scale with the marketplace. Every new seller cohort meant more headcount or a bigger vendor bill.

### Manual review + vendor vs. this system

| Dimension | Manual review + external vendor | AI multi-agent detector |
|---|---|---|
| Per-listing cost | Human analyst hours + per-seller vendor fee | ≈200K Claude tokens — cents per listing |
| Time to verdict | Days to weeks | ~12 minutes end-to-end |
| Throughput | Queue-limited by headcount | Parallel, batch-ready across catalogs |
| Explainability | Analyst notes, ad-hoc write-ups | Cited signals + schema-validated JSON + HTML report |
| DSA Article 30 audit trail | Manually compiled per case | Generated by default on every run |
| Ethics scope (labor / sanctions / environmental) | Separate vendor workflow, if at all | Built-in YAML checklist — 18 rules + `custom_checks:` |
| Scalability | Linear in headcount | Linear in API budget |
| Coverage of new seller cohorts | Reactive — after brand complaints | Proactive — run on every new listing |

Meanwhile the academic literature on counterfeit detection quietly kept advancing. And in parallel, AI agents became good enough to actually *read* a listing page, cross-reference AliExpress, compute perceptual image hashes, and do German-language review NLP — all within the same reasoning session.

So I rebuilt the concept, end-to-end, as an AI-driven system. This article walks through it.

## Why marketplaces have to care: the Digital Services Act

If you run a marketplace in the EU, counterfeit detection is no longer an ethics-and-reputation line item. Since 2024 it is a regulatory one.

The **EU Digital Services Act (DSA)** — not the Digital Markets Act — imposes binding obligations on online marketplaces:

- **Article 30 (Traceability of traders — "Know Your Business" / KYB):** marketplaces must verify seller identity, business registration, and contact information *before* a seller can list.
- **Random product checks:** marketplaces must make "reasonable efforts" to perform random checks against public databases of illegal products, or deploy new technologies for product traceability.
- **Trusted flaggers:** rightsholders and brand protection organizations get priority-reporting channels whose notices must be processed quickly.
- **Penalties:** up to **6% of global annual turnover** for breaches.

For a marketplace doing €10B in GMV, that ceiling is measured in hundreds of millions of euros. Manual review plus a vendor contract was always unsatisfying; under DSA it is also a compliance risk. Automated, cheap, transparent, and auditable detection becomes a business necessity — not a nice-to-have.

### How this system maps to DSA Article 30

| DSA obligation | Where this system delivers it |
|---|---|
| Trader identity & business registration (Art. 30 §1) | `seller-investigator` — KYB probes: *Handelsregister*, *Impressum*, *USt-IdNr.* |
| Traceable contact details (Art. 30 §1) | `seller-investigator` — contact-field extraction and free-mail / shell-company flagging |
| "Reasonable efforts" random product checks | `image-forensics` + `review-analyst` — multi-platform image reuse and review-integrity signals |
| Trusted-flagger notice inputs (Art. 22 linkage) | Verdict JSON — structured evidence + citations, export-ready |
| Audit trail & explainability | `validate_verdict.py` + HTML report — every flag cites its signal and source |
| Scope beyond counterfeit (sanctions, labor, environmental) | `config/seller-ethics-checklist.yaml` — 18 built-in checks + `custom_checks:` slot for proprietary blocklists |

## What the system actually does

The project is a **Claude Code multi-agent system** built on top of the [Claude Agent SDK](https://www.anthropic.com/claude-code). You give it a single input — an Amazon ASIN, an OTTO article number, or a full product URL — and it orchestrates five specialized AI agents in parallel, produces a JSON verdict, validates it against a schema, and generates an HTML report.

### The pipeline

```
Input (URL, ASIN, or article number)
        │
        ▼
  Platform detection
  (Amazon vs OTTO.de)
        │
        ▼
┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│   ◄── Parallel dispatch: 4 agents fire in one message ──► │
│                                                           │
│  product-researcher    review-analyst                     │
│  (listing metadata,    (NLP: German and English           │
│   seller, price,        counterfeit keywords,             │
│   FBA/FBM)              sentiment-vs-stars mismatch)      │
│                                                           │
│  image-forensics       seller-investigator                │
│  (pHash + dHash,       (network analysis, account         │
│   reverse image         age, catalog coherence,           │
│   search on             ethics checklist, KYB signals)    │
│   AliExpress/DHgate/                                      │
│   SHEIN)                                                  │
└───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
        │
        ▼
┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐
│   fake-product-classifier                 │
│   - weighted composite score              │
│   - verdict + confidence                  │
│   - evidence_for / evidence_against lists │
└──────────────────────────────────────────┘
        │
        ▼
   Verdict JSON  ─►  Schema validation  ─►  HTML report
```

Each subagent returns only a **compact contract** — `{path, 3-5 key findings, flag_count}` — never raw HTML or review bodies. This is how the whole investigation fits in a fraction of one Claude Pro session: the orchestrator never ingests the raw scrape. That lesson came out of an earlier version that *did* ingest the raw scrape and burned through my entire session token budget on a single investigation.

### Platform support

| Platform | Command | Product ID format |
|---|---|---|
| Amazon (all TLDs: .com, .de, .co.uk, .fr, ...) | `/investigate [ASIN or URL]` | `B0GQDB5Y1J` |
| OTTO.de | `/investigate [article-number or URL]` | `1789019591` |

The same `/investigate` command auto-detects the platform. Under the hood there are two parallel agent families — one tuned for Amazon (FBA/FBM signals, seller hijacking patterns, ASIN red flags) and one tuned for OTTO.de (German regulatory checks — *Impressum*, *USt-IdNr.*, *Handelsregister* — German NLP, and OTTO's seller taxonomy of *OTTO Versand* vs *Händlerversand*).

## The scientific foundation

This is not "trust me" code. The weights, thresholds, and features are all grounded in peer-reviewed work. I rebuilt the pipeline specifically so it could cite its sources.

### Feature weights (from the `counterfeit-methodology` skill)

| Signal | Weight | Backed by |
|---|---:|---|
| Fake topic score (review keywords) | 0.222 | Cao et al. 2022; Massey 2023 |
| Sentiment mismatch (stars vs text) | 0.167 | Massey 2023 |
| Unauthorized marketplace image match | 0.167 | Cheung et al. 2018; Soldner 2023 |
| Seller network centrality | 0.089 | Cheung et al. 2018 |
| Cross-ASIN / cross-listing image sharing | 0.089 | Cheung et al. 2018 |
| Shared reviewers | 0.078 | Massey 2023 |
| Account age | 0.056 | Cao et al. 2022 |
| Feedback score | 0.056 | Cao et al. 2022 |
| Seller count on listing | 0.044 | Cao et al. 2022 |
| Price variance | 0.033 | Cao et al. 2022 |

### The papers

1. **Cao, Dewan & Lin (2022) — *Identification and Impact of Online Deceptive Counterfeit Products: Evidence from Amazon*.** SSRN 4398423. A Random Forest classifier over listing and review features reached **83% accuracy** on a labeled Amazon dataset. This is the backbone of my fake-topic and rating-distribution signals.

2. **Cheung, She & Liu (2018) — *Deep learning-based online counterfeit-seller detection*.** Showed that treating sellers as nodes in an image-sharing graph (seller A and seller B using the same images) produces a **~60% improvement** over per-listing features in isolation. This is why cross-ASIN and cross-listing image sharing each get their own weight.

3. **Massey (2023) — *Identify Counterfeit Product Reviews or AI Text by Bots on E-commerce Websites*.** National College of Ireland. Showed that a **BERT classifier reaches 97%** on fake-review detection when sentiment-vs-star-rating mismatch is treated as a first-class feature alongside duplicate-cluster detection.

4. **Soldner (2023) — *Combating online consumer fraud and counterfeits: A data science perspective*.** University College London. Multi-platform image matching as a near-definitive signal — the same image on AliExpress or DHgate at a lower price. Also contributed the sobering finding that **expert annotators disagree on "suspicious" labels about 40% of the time**, which is why my system caps confidence at 0.95 and ships an explicit UNCERTAIN verdict bucket.

A `CITATION.cff` file in the repository lets you cite the tool itself, and `references:` inside it lists all four works.

### Verdict thresholds (also research-informed)

| Composite score | Verdict | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| < 0.20 | LIKELY AUTHENTIC | High |
| 0.20 – 0.39 | LIKELY AUTHENTIC | Medium |
| 0.40 – 0.59 | UNCERTAIN | — |
| 0.60 – 0.79 | LIKELY COUNTERFEIT | Medium |
| ≥ 0.80 | LIKELY COUNTERFEIT | High |

## Seller ethics vetting — beyond counterfeits

The seller-investigator agent also runs a **configurable prohibited-practices checklist** (`config/seller-ethics-checklist.yaml`) covering:

- Human rights (child labor, forced labor, wage theft)
- Ideological (extremist ideology, hate speech)
- Animal welfare (exotic leather, fur trade)
- Environmental (deforestation, illegal mining, conflict minerals)
- Legal / sanctions (OFAC, trademark infringement)
- Fraud (fake review schemes, known scam operations)

This checklist is a direct lift from the OTTO-era work. At OTTO Group Fraud Management, a large part of the job was *exactly this*: identifying suppliers that were non-compliant for reasons well beyond counterfeit — child-labor supply chains surfacing in supplier audits, sellers whose catalogs or branding drifted into extremist or Nazi imagery, operations flagged for animal cruelty, sanctions-list entities trying to onboard through shell companies. These were not hypothetical; every one of those categories corresponded to real investigations that a human team had to run, document, and defend. Counterfeit is rarely the only thing wrong with a bad seller, and buyers increasingly care about ethics alongside authenticity.

The checklist captures that institutional knowledge as machine-readable rules. It is YAML-configurable — `custom_checks:` merges seamlessly with the 18 built-in entries — so compliance teams can add their own proprietary criteria (internal blocklists, rightsholder rulings, category-specific bans) without touching agent prompts. Each entry carries a severity and a set of search terms; the seller-investigator runs `WebSearch "{seller_name}" + term` for every entry and flags any corroborated hit into the final verdict.

## A real investigation end-to-end: ASIN B0GQDB5Y1J

Let me walk through the tankini case from the intro, because it shows every tier of the system working together.

**Input.** A single line: `/investigate https://www.amazon.de/dp/B0GQDB5Y1J`.

**Platform detection.** URL contains `amazon.de` → Amazon pipeline. ASIN extracted: `B0GQDB5Y1J`.

**Parallel research phase (4 agents, one message).**

`product-researcher` found:
- 26-word keyword-stuffed German title (a throwaway-listing signature).
- "Hersteller" field literally reads *"Damen Bademode"* — there is no manufacturer.
- FBM dropship. Single-seller ASIN. 51 days old. Country of origin China.
- One review (5 stars, "Sehr schön").

`review-analyst` found:
- Sample size = 1, two-word review, zero statistical signal. Reported that as a finding rather than failing. Important: on a 51-day-old listing, *absence* of complaints is uninformative.

`image-forensics` found:
- **Identical imagery on AliExpress ($6.59–$25.39), DHgate, SHEIN, Made-in-China, and Alibaba CDN.**
- Same imagery reused across other Amazon ASINs under different brand strings (listing-template reuse).
- EXIF metadata fully stripped on all three product images.
- Image-based counterfeit probability: 0.92.

`seller-investigator` found:
- Shenzhen entity with a `@yeah.net` free-mail contact.
- Storefront name embeds emojis and a "50%~80% Rabatt" discount claim — violates Amazon naming policy.
- 558-SKU catalog sprawling across bikinis, fleece jackets, bras, yoga pants, rain ponchos.
- 3.9/5 lifetime, 71% positive over 12 months, 23% one-star, recent feedback describing return-fraud.
- No German *Handelsregister*.

**Classifier.** Loaded the `counterfeit-methodology` and `amazon-patterns` skills, applied the Clothing/Footwear category upweight on cross-platform image match, computed composite = **0.85**, verdict = **LIKELY COUNTERFEIT**, risk = **HIGH**, confidence = 0.85 (capped below 0.95 because three review-tier signals were uninterpretable at n=1 — the system explicitly reported and priced this in).

**Validate + report.** `validate_verdict.py` confirmed the JSON schema; `generate_report.py` produced a standalone HTML file with every signal, finding, and the ethics-checklist outcome.

## Embedding the verdict HTML in a Medium article

This is a subtle point. **Medium does not support arbitrary HTML or `<iframe>` embeds** in posts — their editor strips custom styles and script for security. So you cannot literally paste `verdicts/B0GQDB5Y1J_report.html` into a Medium draft and have it render.

Three things you *can* do:

1. **Screenshot the report** and embed it as an image. Cleanest option. Take a full-page screenshot (in Chrome: DevTools → device toolbar → "Capture full size screenshot") and upload it like any image. Good for one hero visual per article.
2. **Host the HTML on GitHub Pages and link to it.** Put `verdicts/B0GQDB5Y1J_report.html` in a `/reports/` directory, enable Pages on the repo, and link `https://<user>.github.io/<repo>/reports/B0GQDB5Y1J_report.html` from the article. Medium auto-renders the link as a clickable preview card.
3. **Use a GitHub Gist for the verdict JSON.** Medium *does* natively embed Gists (paste the gist URL on its own line). Works great for showing the structured verdict data. The full HTML template is too big for a gist to be nice, but the JSON is perfect.

For this article I would use **option 1 for the visual summary** (screenshot of the rendered report, maybe cropped to the headline gauge + risk flags table) and **option 3 for reproducibility** (gist the verdict JSON so readers can see the exact schema).

If you want to publish the article on GitHub directly (a repo `README`, a GitHub Pages site, or an `ARTICLE.md` file), none of these limitations apply — GitHub renders inline HTML in Markdown, so the report can be linked or even iframe'd from a Pages site.

## Token efficiency was not optional

An earlier version of this system burned through an entire Claude Pro session on **one** investigation — not acceptable for anyone planning to open-source it. I had to redesign for token economy without hurting classification quality. What worked:

- **Parallelism.** Four research agents fire in a single orchestrator message, not sequentially.
- **Compact return contract.** Each subagent returns `{path, key_findings, flag_count}` — never raw HTML or review text. The artifact lives on disk; the orchestrator reads only the summary.
- **`curl` + Python parse.** For HTML-heavy fetches, `curl -o file.html` followed by a targeted parse costs a fraction of `WebFetch` because the model never ingests the full page.
- **No recursive fanout.** Leaf agents do not get the `Task` tool — they cannot spawn further subagents. This was a sneaky token sink in version one.
- **Gated hooks.** The pre-prompt context-loader hook was unconditionally firing on every turn. Deleted.
- **Skills, not inlined knowledge.** The 300-line scoring methodology used to live in the classifier's prompt. Now it lives in a skill file the classifier loads on demand.

Post-redesign, a full investigation costs a small fraction of a single session.

## Limitations — because science demands them

- **Pre-purchase analysis only.** Physical verification still requires the product in hand.
- **Published accuracies (83% Cao, 97% Massey) are dataset-specific.** Real-world performance varies by category, marketplace, and adversarial pressure.
- **Ground truth is noisy** (40% expert disagreement per Soldner 2023). This is why I ship an explicit UNCERTAIN bucket instead of forcing a binary.
- **Counterfeiters adapt.** Every public detection paper is also a roadmap for evasion.
- **Image-generation AI** will eventually defeat perceptual hashing. The fix — embedding-based nearest-neighbor search over CLIP or similar — is on the roadmap.
